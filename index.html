Bring (makes life easier)

  • Walking gear: broken-in shoes; blister plasters; thin socks you can change midday at parks.
  • Toddler reality: compact stroller or carrier, lightweight rain cover, more wipes than you think, spare outfit + plastic bags for messes.
  • Power: compact power bank(s), short USB cables; Japan uses Type A/B outlets at 100V—many U.S. plugs work; still pack a small adapter if any device needs it.
  • Comfort on planes: kid headphones, loaded tablets, small snacks, empty water bottles to fill after security, eye masks for the overnight leg.
  • Weather: light packable rain layer per person; sun hats; small umbrella.
  • Organization: packing cubes or zip bags per person; one pouch for passports, Suica/IC cards, and pen for forms.
  • Health kit: your usual kid fever/pain relief (labeled), motion sickness if anyone needs it, bandages, any eczema or allergy meds you trust.

Do not bring (or avoid overdoing)

  • Japan-illegal or risky items: firearms, knives not allowed as tools, pepper spray, and cannabis or CBD products—even traces in oils or edibles can create serious legal trouble.
  • Medications: avoid packing large quantities of unfamiliar OTC cold meds; some ingredients (e.g. certain stimulants or codeine rules) are controlled—when in doubt, check official import guidance and keep prescriptions documented.
  • Bulk: full-size shampoo and soap (buy at conbini or use hotel); heavy guidebooks; multiple pairs of “just in case” shoes.
  • Single point of failure: one giant checked bag for the whole family—split essentials across bags so one delayed suitcase does not strand everyone.
  • Park day mistakes: brand-new shoes; huge non-collapsible stroller if trains will be crowded; toys that are easy to lose and hard to replace mid-meltdown.

Disney Premier Access & ride scheduling

  • Paid “fast lane” here is Disney Premier Access: buy timed entry or shorter-wait access for select rides and some parades/shows through the Tokyo Disney Resort app (fees and eligible experiences change—check the official page and in-app list on your park day).
  • Before the trip: install the app, create/link My Disney accounts for anyone who might purchase Premier Access, and confirm everyone’s park tickets are tied to profiles in the app so one adult isn’t stuck doing everything at gate time.
  • Family of 6 economics: Premier Access is usually per guest, per experience—costs add up fast. Pick one or two true “must-dos” that tend to spike (often the newest or flashiest rides) rather than buying every offering.
  • Pacing: after entering, pick return windows that fit meals, parade/show times, and stroller naps. Assign one parent as “app captain” to grab windows so the rest of the group isn’t scanning phones at every turnstile.
  • Standby strategy: rope-drop one compact land cluster, do 1–2 short-queue rides while lines are thin, then either standby your single top priority early or switch to Premier Access if waits are exploding—avoid zig-zagging across the park for marginal savings.
  • On crowded days the app may also use Standby Pass / entry rules for certain experiences—treat the app’s prompts as the authority, not third-party blogs.
  • How to buy Disney Premier Access (step-by-step): after every ticket has entered the park, the app captain opens the official Tokyo Disney Resort app → Disney Premier Access → pick an eligible attraction/show → select every guest who will ride (height rules per kid) → choose a return time that still fits meals, parade picks, and stroller naps → pay in-app. Re-check the list hourly—new inventory sometimes appears, but don’t chase tiny gains across the park.
  • What to buy first: use Premier Access on the one “unicorn” headliner you did not rope-drop, or whichever eligible ride is posting the longest stable waits on the app (summer peaks often cluster on Beauty and the Beast “Magical Tale”, Pooh’s Hunny Hunt, Monsters, Inc. Ride & Go Seek, and the big mountains). Buying Premier for a ride you can still walk onto in 20 minutes wastes the budget—standby those and save paid access for a true time sink.
